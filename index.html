Flood Map Fundamentals

FIRM — Flood Insurance Rate Map
  • Most common FEMA flood map product; panel maps combine hazard zones with base-map features such as streets, landmarks, and community boundaries.
  • Regulatory map used by communities for permitting under 44 CFR 60.3
  • Zones: AE = detailed study with BFE; A = approximate, no BFE; AH = 1-3 ft shallow ponding with BFE; AO = 1-3 ft shallow sheet flow with average depth shown; AR = levee/flood-control restoration area; A99 = federal flood-control system under construction; V = coastal wave action, no BFE; VE = coastal wave action with BFE; X-shaded = 500-yr / moderate flood hazard; X-unshaded = minimal hazard; D = possible but undetermined hazard
  • Legacy FIRM labels: A1-30 maps to modern AE; V1-30 maps to modern VE; B maps to shaded X; C maps to unshaded X.
  • FIRM panels updated piecemeal — not a unified national snapshot
  • Effective date can be 5–30+ years old for rural areas
  • FIS (Flood Insurance Study) = engineering report that underlies the FIRM; contains the hydraulic model, frequency analysis, cross-sections, and BFE profiles
  • FIRM = regulatory product; BLE = non-regulatory screening tool (unless state converts it)
  • Post-Risk Rating 2.0 cue: the FIRM still controls regulation and SFHA-based mandatory purchase, but it is no longer the sole or controlling factor for NFIP premium pricing.
FEMA-Mapped Flood Categories — Riverine, Shallow, Coastal

For FIRM/FIS work, FEMA commonly groups mapped flood risk into three primary categories. This is a mapping-product taxonomy, not a complete physical-risk taxonomy.

CategoryTypical FIRM expressionPractical meaning
RiverineA / AE zones, BFEs, flood profiles, floodwaysFlooding from rivers, streams, and channels. The FIS profile and cross sections are usually the key technical record.
ShallowAO / AH zones; sheet flow or pondingFlooding generally mapped by depth or shallow ponding rather than a full river profile. AO is shown as a DEPTH value; AH is shown as an EL/BFE value. Important for local drainage, alluvial fans, and flat urban or low-gradient areas.
CoastalV / VE zones, coastal A zones, coastal transectsFlooding driven by coastal water levels, wave action, runup, erosion, and debris impact; foundations and floodproofing rules are much stricter.
Workshop cue: do not equate FEMA's "shallow" category with all pluvial flooding. AO/AH zones capture some sheet-flow and ponding hazards, but many urban drainage, storm-sewer surcharge, and manhole-overflow failures still sit outside mapped SFHA boundaries.
How FIRMs Are Built — Data Sources and Remote Sensing
  • FIRMs are not made by simply tracing floodwater from satellite images. They are regulatory products built from Flood Insurance Studies, topographic data, hydrologic analysis, hydraulic models, and local review.
  • Core inputs: LiDAR or other topographic mapping, field survey, stream and drainage data, bridge/culvert/levee information, hydrologic frequency analysis, hydraulic modeling such as HEC-RAS, and base map GIS layers.
  • Remote sensing role: satellite imagery and aerial imagery can support land-cover interpretation, impervious surface estimates, change detection, historical flood documentation, and post-disaster validation.
  • Important distinction: LiDAR is often airborne remote sensing, not satellite data. It is usually more important than satellite imagery for regulatory flood elevation and floodplain boundary work.
  • Exam/workshop cue: Satellite data can help understand risk, but BFE and SFHA boundaries normally depend on surveyed/topographic data plus hydrologic and hydraulic modeling.
NFHL — National Flood Hazard Layer
  • FEMA's GIS database aggregating all effective FIRM data nationwide
  • Covers >90% of U.S. population
  • Updated continuously with each new effective FIRM and each LOMC
  • NOT a unified snapshot — every polygon references its own effective date and study method
  • Critical limitation: querying NFHL gives you the current regulatory zone, not the current physical risk
  • Accessible via FEMA's National Flood Map Service Center and ArcGIS REST services

Approximate Zone A — Estimating a BFE

Zone A has no published BFE. For screening-level work, a simplified BFE can be estimated from topographic contours, but final permitting should use best available local, FEMA, or engineering data.

MethodHow it worksUse with care
Contour interpolationOverlay the Zone A floodplain boundary on a topographic contour map, matching map scale. Check whether the left and right floodplain banks follow contour lines within acceptable limits.If bank elevations differ by more than about half the contour interval, the estimate is weak.
BFE estimateIf acceptable, use the lower floodplain-boundary water-surface elevation plus one-half contour interval. Example: 597 ft + 5 ft = 602 ft for a 10-ft contour interval.This is an approximate BFE, not a detailed-study BFE.
GIS contour interpolationUse DEM/contours and mapped Zone A boundaries to check bank elevations and calculate the same lower-elevation + half-interval estimate.Output quality depends on terrain data resolution, datum, and floodplain boundary accuracy.
Data extrapolationExtend nearby detailed-study flood profiles beyond the detailed study area when appropriate. Use only within about 500 ft upstream of a detailed-study Zone AE reach and only where channel characteristics remain similar.Do not extrapolate through major hydraulic changes such as dams, waterfalls, bridges, culverts, confluences, reservoirs, or abrupt slope changes; avoid areas with downstream backwater effects.
Best available dataUse available federal, state, and local data for Approximate Zone A areas.Data should reasonably reflect flooding conditions, be technically correct, represent the best available information, and be documented with its source.

ASPRS Positional Accuracy Standards Ed. 2 V2 (2024)
  • Published 2024; supersedes 2014 edition
  • Key change 1: Primary metric is now RMSEz — 95% confidence interval factor eliminated as primary metric
  • Key change 2: Minimum checkpoints increased from 20 to 30
  • Key change 3: Checkpoint distribution rules tightened (vegetated vs. open terrain)
  • Vertical Accuracy Classes: RMSEz ≤5 cm, 10 cm, 20 cm, etc.

  • Vegetated Vertical Accuracy (VVA) = under canopy — always worse than NVA; reported at 95th percentile
  • Why it matters: FEMA's lidar acceptance spec references ASPRS standards; a 2014-spec survey delivered in 2025 may not meet the updated standard

Base Level Engineering (BLE)

BLE — What It Is and What It Isn't
  • Automated 1D/2D HEC-RAS modeling at HUC-8 or HUC-12 scale using QL2 lidar terrain
  • Produced by FEMA contractors for screening/prioritization — NOT regulatory
  • Key limitation: no surveyed channel bathymetry → BFEs can be off by 1–3 ft
  • No field calibration required → model not validated against observed events
  • Some states have converted BLE to regulatory: Kansas KDA has; most have not
  • BLE is the starting point for community outreach — don't let it become the endpoint
  • BLE shows approximate floodplain extent; communities should commission detailed studies for regulated areas
Critical framing: BLE being shown as a "preliminary" map to communities who then assume it IS the regulatory map is a documented source of planning confusion. Raise this in discussion.

2D Modeling Pitfalls (Know These Cold)
  1. Mesh resolution: Too coarse = overestimates flood extent; too fine = computational instability. Rule of thumb: mesh cells ≤ 1/3 channel width
  2. Boundary conditions: Upstream flow hydrograph + downstream stage or normal depth. Wrong downstream BC = wrong backwater profile
  3. Terrain preprocessing: Must "burn" channel bathymetry into lidar DEM; must remove bridges/culverts from terrain (then re-add as structures)
  4. Manning's n calibration: Land cover-based n values without field calibration are educated guesses; ±20% uncertainty in peak BFE
  5. Bridge/culvert representation: HEC-RAS bridge routines work in 1D; in 2D you must use SA/2D connection or inline structures
  6. Calibration requirement: At least 2 independent flood events with observed WSEs and discharges; single-event calibration is insufficient
  7. Unsteady vs. steady: Unsteady better for dynamic floodplain; steady simpler but misses timing-related effects

Datum Transition: NAVD88 → NAPGD2022

Why This Matters to Every FIRM in America
  • NAVD88 (North American Vertical Datum of 1988) = current national vertical reference for all FIRMs, BFEs, elevation certificates
  • Known errors: biased ~0.5 m nationally; coast-to-coast tilt of ~1 m (higher in east, lower in west)
  • NAPGD2022 (North American-Pacific Geodetic Datum of 2022) = replacement; FGCS vote expected mid-2026
  • Transition will NOT happen overnight — FEMA, USGS, NOAA all need to update products
  • Implication: Every FIRM, BFE, and elevation certificate referencing NAVD88 will eventually need datum transformation; properties currently "above BFE" may shift status
  • Practical planning: communities near coasts where NAVD88 error is largest should begin assessing impact now
  • Elevation certificates should note datum; surveyors doing ECs now should be aware of future transformation requirement
The FGCS vote expected mid-2026 means this topic is live at the conference. Expect it to come up in workshop discussion.

Future of Flood Risk Data (FFRD)

FFRD — Probabilistic Risk Products
  • Joint USACE-FEMA Interagency Research Working Agreement (IRWA)
  • Goal: replace binary SFHA/non-SFHA with probabilistic, graduated risk products
  • Return period range: ~5-year to 2,000-year
  • Method: Monte Carlo via HEC-WAT (Watershed Analysis Tool) + ensemble hydraulic modeling
  • Three pilot watersheds currently underway (locations vary; check FEMA 2026 updates)
  • Non-regulatory at this time — not replacing FIRMs yet
  • If implemented: could transform NFIP pricing, CRS, and building code triggers
  • Biggest challenge: regulatory framework is binary (SFHA/non-SFHA); probabilistic products require legislative or rulemaking change

GIS / Geospatial Decision Making

How GIS Decisions Propagate Downstream

Every GIS decision—coordinate system, projection, datum, raster resolution, vectorization method, attribute schema—cascades into regulatory outcomes:

  1. Projection errors: Using a geographic CRS instead of projected CRS in HEC-RAS introduces systematic distance errors; cross-sections may be misaligned
  2. DEM resolution: 1m DEM vs. 3m DEM yields measurably different floodplain extents; FEMA requires ≤3m for detailed studies
  3. Datum confusion: Mixing NAVD88 and NGVD29 terrains without transformation is a documented source of BFE errors (can be 1-3 ft)
  4. Hydrologic connectivity: Sinks, flat areas, and bridges must be handled correctly in terrain preprocessing; automated fill-sink routines sometimes create phantom drainage paths
  5. Attribution errors: NFHL polygon attributes (zone designation, BFE, effective date) can be inconsistent across county boundaries due to stitching of different effective FIRMs
Workshop 1 key insight to share: The gap between QL2 lidar accuracy (10 cm RMSEz) and the 1-foot freeboard in most ordinances suggests communities are managing to a false precision. When BLE BFEs can be off by 1–3 ft due to missing bathymetry, the 1-foot surcharge standard is illusory. Push for mandatory bathymetric surveys in detailed study watersheds.

Workshop 2: Nonstructural Mitigation

June 1, 8:00am-Noon Central · $50 additional cost · "Nonstructural Mitigation for Pluvial, Fluvial, and Coastal Flood Events" — Behm, Shaw, Scott, Elvir

Core Argument of This Workshop

Structural measures (levees, floodwalls) are expensive, often inequitable, and create residual risk. Nonstructural approaches—elevation, relocation, buyouts, floodproofing, early warning—offer flexible, scalable, property-specific solutions that reduce both risk and federal fiscal exposure. The challenge is matching the right tool to each flood mechanism.

The Unresolved Tension (Know This)

NFIP PROHIBITS dry floodproofing of residential structures in the SFHA. (44 CFR 60.3)

Dry floodproofing of non-residential buildings in A zones = allowed. Dry floodproofing in V zones = NEVER (wave action would destroy seals).

No rulemaking to change the residential prohibition is pending. This is the most contested topic in Workshop 2's universe. Presenters may propose policy changes — be ready to probe the evidence base.

Coastal Flooding — Surge, Runup, Erosion, and Higher Standards
  • Primary drivers: storm surge, wave runup, severe-storm water levels, and in lake systems, wind setup and seiche.
  • Transect logic: coastal analysis runs from offshore to onshore across shoreline, dunes, vegetation, buildings, overland wind fetch, and the inland limit of tidal flooding and waves.
  • Wave-height thresholds: VE / coastal high hazard area generally means breaking-wave height ≥3 ft. Coastal A Zone means wave height below the V-zone threshold but still damaging; LiMWA marks the inland limit of the ≥1.5 ft moderate-wave-action area.
  • Coastal erosion matters: beaches, dunes, bluffs, vegetation, sand characteristics, waves, and wind can change the protective landform before or during a flood.
  • Human activities matter: seawalls, dredging, shoreline armoring, fill, and navigation projects can shift erosion, wave reflection, sediment transport, and flood exposure.
  • Not just ocean coasts: coastal-style flood hazards also apply around the Great Lakes and large inland water bodies such as the Great Salt Lake.
  • Minimum NFIP standards are not maximum safety standards: they regulate the base flood framework, but do not guarantee protection from extremely large, rare, compound, or rapidly changing coastal events.
  • Communities can adopt higher standards: larger freeboard, erosion setbacks, V-zone-style construction in coastal A zones, lower substantial-damage thresholds, and stricter limits on fill or shoreline armoring.
Workshop cue: for coastal and Great Lakes sites, ask whether the chosen mitigation standard addresses only mapped base-flood water level, or also wave action, runup, erosion, debris impact, and future shoreline change.
Map-reading cue: the LiMWA line separates coastal A Zone areas with moderate wave action from inland AE areas. It is not the same as the VE boundary: VE is the high-hazard wave area, while LiMWA identifies where ≥1.5 ft waves stop.

Damage Modes — Forces of Water and Prolonged Contact

How Floodwater Actually Damages Buildings

Flood source tells you where water comes from; damage mode tells you what the building must resist or tolerate. This is the bridge between flood mechanism taxonomy and the mitigation toolbox.

Damage modeWhat it doesMitigation implication
Buoyant force / flotationUplifts tanks, slabs, lightweight structures, and enclosed spaces when water pressure exceeds dead load or anchorage.Anchor tanks and utilities; avoid sealed below-grade enclosures that can float or fail.
Hydrostatic forceStill water pushes laterally on walls and vertically on floors; unequal inside/outside water levels can cause collapse.Wet floodproofing uses flood openings to equalize pressure; dry floodproofing must be engineered for the design floodproofing height.
Hydrodynamic forceMoving water adds lateral load, scour, and impact-like pressure, especially in fast fluvial or flash-pluvial flow.Elevation, relocation, open foundations, and avoiding obstructive encroachment are more reliable than relying on wall seals.
Debris impactFloating cars, lumber, ice, trees, tanks, and street debris can puncture walls, barriers, doors, and glazing.Deployable barriers and floodproof glazing need impact assumptions; coastal/V zones and high-velocity flow are poor candidates for dry floodproofing.
Water damage / contaminationProlonged contact weakens structural integrity, destroys nonstructural components, ruins drywall, and promotes mold.Use flood-resistant materials below BFE, elevate utilities, and plan for cleanup access; do not confuse shallow water with low damage.
Workshop cue: wet floodproofing accepts water entry to prevent hydrostatic collapse and then limits damage with flood-resistant materials. Dry floodproofing tries to keep water out, so it must answer a harder question: can the building, openings, barriers, O&M plan, warning lead time, and debris exposure all perform together?

Prolonged Water Contact

  • Weakens a building's structural integrity, especially when wood framing, connections, subfloors, or masonry assemblies stay wet.
  • Destroys nonstructural components such as gypsum board, cabinets, finishes, insulation, electrical devices, and HVAC components.
  • Drywall is usually ruined once saturated; flood cuts and replacement are often more realistic than drying in place.
  • Mold can develop in walls, ductwork, and insulation when moisture remains trapped after visible water recedes.

Why This Matters for WS2

  • Elevation: avoids both forces and prolonged contact by keeping occupiable floors above the damaging water level.
  • Wet floodproofing: manages hydrostatic force, but accepts cleanup and material replacement as part of the design logic.
  • Dry floodproofing: reduces contact damage only if barriers are deployed in time and are strong enough for water pressure and debris exposure.
  • EWS: useful only when warning lead time exceeds the time needed to install barriers, move contents, shut down utilities, and evacuate.

Nonstructural Mitigation Toolbox

Elevation (Freeboard / Raise Lowest Floor)
  • Most reliable long-term mitigation; reduces damage geometrically with elevation above BFE
  • NFIP rate reductions: 1 ft above BFE = ~30% premium reduction; 2 ft = ~60%; 3 ft = ~70%+
  • Practical limit: foundation type (slab, crawlspace, basement) determines cost and feasibility
  • Wisconsin NR 116: FPE = BFE + 2 ft (state freeboard = most stringent in Midwest)
  • FEMA Hazard Mitigation Grant Program funds elevation for SRL/RL properties
  • Acquisition preferred over elevation for properties ≥50% damaged (Substantial Damage trigger)
Relocation & Acquisition / Buyouts
  • Relocation: Move structure out of floodplain; preserves building; expensive; requires suitable alternate site
  • Acquisition: Purchase property at pre-flood fair market value; demolish; deed-restrict as open space; most permanent solution
  • FEMA requires acquired parcels remain open space in perpetuity (44 CFR 80)
  • Voluntary only — no eminent domain under HMGP/FMA
  • Key equity issue: buyout programs can displace low-income renters without compensating them; owners paid but renters lose housing
  • Swift Current program ($500M): expedited buyouts for SRL/RL properties post-disaster
  • Community resistance: "shrinkage" fears, tax base loss, neighborhood dissolution
Dry Floodproofing — Rules, Limits, ANSI/FM 2510

Definition: Make the building watertight below the DFE using barriers, sealed walls, waterproof coatings, check valves. Water does NOT enter.

  • Allowed: Non-residential structures in A zones (AE, A, AO, AH) — 44 CFR 60.3(c)(3)
  • NOT allowed: Residential structures in SFHA — 44 CFR 60.3 (no exception; no rulemaking pending)
  • NEVER: V zones (coastal wave action would destroy any seal)
  • Structural limit: FEMA P-936 limits application to ≤3 ft of flooding above BFE for most structures
  • Hydrostatic pressure at 3 ft water depth = 187 lb/ft²; at 6 ft = 375 lb/ft²
  • ANSI/FM 2510 (2020): Only U.S. national consensus standard for flood mitigation equipment — covers perimeter barriers, opening barriers, backwater valves, flood glazing
  • FEMA deployable barrier requirement in ASCE 24-24: written O&M plan + annual deployment drills + ANSI/FM 2510 compliance
The residential prohibition is the elephant in the room. Presenters from US Flood Control (Elvir) sell commercial flood barriers — watch for implicit arguments that the residential prohibition should be relaxed. Push back with: what is the failure rate data? What happens when barriers fail and residents stayed?

Wet Floodproofing

Definition: Allow water to enter; use flood-resistant materials and design to minimize damage. Water DOES enter.

  • Required for enclosed areas below BFE in residential structures (with flood openings)
  • Typically used for: crawlspaces, attached garages, below-grade parking, accessory structures
  • Flood openings (vents): must provide 1 sq in net open area per sq ft of enclosed space (NFIP standard) OR engineered alternative
  • FEMA Technical Bulletin 1 (2021): flood opening sizing requirements
  • FEMA Technical Bulletin 7 (2022): wet floodproofing design
  • Flood-resistant materials: FEMA TB-2 (2025 revision): Class 4 and 5 materials; concrete, CMU, ceramic tile, pressure-treated wood, closed-cell foam — can be submerged 72 hrs without significant damage
  • Does NOT satisfy NFIP minimum compliance for the main structure — only for ancillary spaces
Early Warning Systems (EWS)
  • Sensor network (rain gauges, stream gauges, soil moisture) + alert dissemination + community response protocol
  • USGS StreamStats / National Water Information System: real-time gauge data
  • NOAA Advanced Hydrologic Prediction Service (AHPS): 1-7 day forecasts at 4,000+ gauge locations
  • Texas Hill Country July 4, 2025: 139+ deaths despite gauge network — shows EWS alone insufficient without evacuation plan + public action
  • Key components: (1) detection, (2) forecast, (3) alert dissemination, (4) community action protocol
  • Preparedness detail: residents and property managers must be enrolled in the local emergency alert system before the event; a technically good alert system fails if the target population never receives or understands the alert.
  • Equity concern: alerts in English only exclude non-English speakers; cell towers may fail in same storm
  • Integration with dry floodproofing: ANSI/FM 2510 / ASCE 24-24 require O&M plans including deployment trigger (flood warning stage)
  • FEMA BRIC funded many EWS projects before termination; status of funded projects unclear
Flood Openings — Technical Requirements
  • Required in all enclosed areas below BFE for NFIP compliance (residential)
  • Standard: 1 in² net open area per 1 ft² of enclosed floor area; minimum 2 openings; openings within 1 ft of grade
  • Engineered openings: must be certified by PE; can use smaller area if designed to equalize pressure faster
  • Automatic openings (ICC-ES/ANSI 1209): rated to open at ≤12 in of water depth; close when water recedes; do not require manual operation
  • Covers: must be removable or automatically open; screen mesh counts against net area if flow restricted
  • FEMA TB-1 (2021) is the authoritative reference

ASCE 24-24 — New Building Standard

Four Key Changes from ASCE 24-14 to ASCE 24-24
  1. Flood Design Class (FDC) replaces simple occupancy categories:
    • FDC 1: Low-risk (ag storage, temporary)
    • FDC 2: Normal (typical residential, commercial)
    • FDC 3: High-risk (schools, fire stations, large assembly)
    • FDC 4: Essential facilities (hospitals, EOCs, critical infrastructure)
  2. Design Flood Elevation (DFE) now FDC-dependent:
    • FDC 1: 100-year MRI (1% annual chance) — same as before
    • FDC 2: 500-year MRI (0.2% annual chance) — major upgrade from 100-yr
    • FDC 3: 750-year MRI (0.13% annual chance)
    • FDC 4: 1,000-year MRI (0.1% annual chance)
  3. 500-year flood hazard area applies to FDC 2/3/4 buildings (FDC 2 design elevation also raised to 500-yr)
  4. Deployable barriers (temporary barriers): If used, must comply with ANSI/FM 2510; written O&M plan required; annual deployment drills required
ASCE 24-24 is NOT yet referenced in the 2024 International Building Code / International Residential Code. Target adoption: 2027 I-Code cycle. Until then, it is a best-practice reference, not a code requirement — unless local jurisdiction has adopted it independently.

Benefit-Cost Analysis (BCA)

FEMA BCA Toolkit & Key Numbers
  • Discount rate: 7% (OMB Circular A-94) — this is the rate at which future benefits are discounted to present value
  • Median riverine flood mitigation BCR: $7:1 (NIBS 2017 study; often cited as $6 per $1 invested)
  • HMGP and BRIC require BCR ≥ 1.0 for eligibility; FMA projects often need BCR ≥ 1.5 in practice
  • BCA benefit categories: avoided property damage, displacement costs (temporary housing), loss of function, mental stress / fatality risk, ecosystem services
  • Social equity benefits: removed by current administration; previously counted for BRIC
  • Critical BCA sensitivity: discount rate. At 7%, a future $1M benefit in year 30 is worth only $131K today. At 3%, it's $412K. Choice of rate is a political decision disguised as an accounting one.
  • BCA for elevation: straightforward using depth-damage functions. BCA for EWS: harder — must estimate lives saved and displacement avoided.

ICC is worth adding because it is the insurance mechanism that can turn a substantial-damage or repetitive-damage determination into partial funding for nonstructural mitigation. It is not a grant, and it is not automatic owner assistance: it is SFIP Coverage D, adjusted separately from the physical flood-damage claim.

FIMP Case Study — Fire Island to Montauk Point

The USACE New York District FIMP nonstructural program is a concrete example of the workshop toolbox becoming an implementation program.

  • Scale: ASFPM describes FIMP as elevating and floodproofing 4,000+ structures in the 10-year floodplain along 83 miles of New York shoreline.

Hydrologic Analysis — From Stage Records to Flood Discharge

Hydrologic analysis estimates how much water is flowing during flood events. In FIS/FIRM work, it provides the discharge inputs that hydraulic models then translate into water-surface elevations, BFEs, floodways, and inundation boundaries.

Development and Permit Triggers

NFIP Definition of Development — Broader Than Buildings

Under NFIP rules, development means a human-made change to improved or unimproved real estate. The practical test is whether the activity changes land, structures, flow paths, storage, or obstruction in the floodplain.

  • Clearly development: buildings, homes, bridge construction, manufactured home parks, RV parks, utilities, roads, and other structures
  • Earthwork and surface changes: filling, grading, paving, excavation, drilling, dredging, mining, and moving dirt
  • Storage can count: equipment, materials, lumber yards, temporary staging areas, debris, or fill stockpiles in the floodplain
  • Public/open-space sites are not exempt: city parks can still involve development if they add structures, grading, fill, trails, utilities, or material storage
  • Exam cue: If the activity is a human-made change in the floodplain, assume it needs floodplain review unless the ordinance clearly excludes it.
This is why floodplain administrators review more than houses. A lumber yard, bridge project, dirt-moving operation, RV/mobile home park, park improvement, or temporary material storage can all affect flood levels or obstruction.

Can I repair? Do I need a permit? Can I stay? What happens if my building is over 50%?

Workshop cue: ask whether communities are maintaining a pre-populated building inventory and substantial damage management plan, or whether they are waiting until after the flood to assemble values, inspectors, forms, and public messaging.
Temporary occupancy cue: decisions and laws vary by community. Some communities do not allow temporary occupancy or emergency repairs at all. Where they are allowed, treat them as controlled emergency permissions, not approval to rebuild below current floodplain standards.

Development Permit Process — Completeness vs. Compliance

A floodplain development permit review has two different gates. First, the community decides whether the application package is complete enough to review. Second, it decides whether the proposed development technically complies with the ordinance, FIRM/FIS, BFE/floodway data, and any higher local standards.

StepDecision pointWhat can happen
1. Applicant prepares packagePlans, site information, proposed work description, elevations, technical documents, certifications, and other required approvals are assembled.Applicant may need pre-application guidance, especially for Zone A, floodway, V-zone, SI/SD, or nonstructural retrofit projects.
2. Submission and intakeCommunity records the submission and collects required fees.Creates the permit record; do not treat informal emails or concept drawings as an approved permit.
3. Completeness reviewCheck whether required plans, technical documents, federal/state/local permit information, department signoffs, and certified documents are present.Incomplete applications are returned for missing information before technical approval or denial.
4. Technical compliance reviewCheck the proposal against floodplain ordinance standards, FIRM/FIS data, BFE, floodway/no-rise rules, elevation/floodproofing requirements, SI/SD determinations, and local higher standards.Noncompliant proposals are returned for correction or denied if they cannot be made compliant.
5. Approve or denyCommunity makes a documented decision based on the complete record.Approval should include enforceable conditions; denial should explain the deficiency and appeal or resubmission path if one exists.
6. Issue permit and inspectPermit is issued only after completeness and compliance gates are satisfied.Later inspections verify that construction matches the approved plans and permit conditions.
Workshop cue: an "incomplete" package is not the same as a "noncompliant" proposal. Missing elevation certificates, engineering certifications, other agency permits, site plans, or department approvals stop the review; technical conflicts with BFE, floodway, dry/wet floodproofing, or SI/SD rules stop approval.
Technical Compliance Review — What Must Be Checked

Technical compliance is the point where the community decides whether the proposed development can legally and physically work in the mapped or known flood hazard setting. It sits between completeness review and permit issuance, and it should drive building inspections and enforcement conditions.

Review areaWhat to checkWhy it matters
Site informationConfirm parcel, legal description, FIRM panel, effective date, flood zone, BFE or AO depth, datum, floodway status, LiMWA/Coastal A cue, LAG/HAG, proposed fill, grading, drainage paths, utilities, access, and whether other local hazard layers apply.A technically complete plan can still be wrong if it uses the wrong panel, datum, flood source, or site boundary. This is the WS1 mapping-to-permit link.
BFE increasesBefore floodway designation, check whether development in A1-30/AE areas would cumulatively raise the base-flood water-surface elevation more than 1 ft. Flood openingsFor wet-floodproofed enclosures and attached garages below BFE, check area, count, location, wall distribution, bottom height, and engineered-opening documentation. Inspection turns paper compliance into actual compliance.
Enforcement pathIf work starts without a permit, deviates from approved plans, blocks openings, encloses prohibited areas, installs low utilities, adds unapproved fill, or fails final elevation checks, use local enforcement tools: stop-work, notice of violation, correction order, withheld final approval, penalties, or legal action as allowed locally.Community enforcement is the front line. FEMA program oversight matters when community enforcement failure becomes systemic, but it is not the normal first response to one project.
Workshop cue: technical compliance is the practical bridge between WS1 and WS2. WS1 supplies the data quality questions: BFE, datum, floodway, terrain, model limits. WS2 supplies the building-response questions: elevation, openings, dry/wet floodproofing, utilities, relocation, and SI/SD triggers.

The application review decision is not the end of floodplain administration. 44 CFR 60.1(d) — Higher Standards Authority

44 CFR 60.3 is the NFIP participation floor, not the ceiling. Under 44 CFR 60.1(d), communities may exceed the minimum criteria by adopting more comprehensive floodplain management regulations.

TopicPractical meaning
Legal postureStates and communities can adopt stricter local standards when they want safer flood-prone development, lower future damage, CRS credit, or rules tailored to local risk.
Common examplesFreeboard, regulating outside the SFHA, compensatory storage, critical-facility protection, lower SI/SD thresholds, cumulative SI/SD tracking, repetitive-loss triggers, erosion/bluff setbacks, and stricter construction standards.
SI/SD higher standardsCommunities can require compliance when improvements or repairs cumulatively reach 50% over a set period such as 5 or 10 years, when multiple damage events add up to 50%, or when a property meets the NFIP repetitive-loss definition.
Lower thresholdA community may set the substantial improvement or substantial damage trigger below 50%, bringing older homes into compliance after smaller projects or less severe damage.
Historic structuresLocal higher standards can require historic structures to meet SI/SD requirements, or require compliance to the extent it does not preclude continued historic designation.
Building-code connectionInternational Codes and ASCE 24 often add more detailed flood-resistant design rules than the NFIP text alone, including flood openings, V-zone-style construction in Coastal A Zones, and manufactured-home foundation elevation.
ASCE 24 cautionASCE 24 is referenced through the I-Code framework, but adopted edition matters. This dossier treats ASCE 24-24 as a forward-looking best-practice reference unless a jurisdiction has independently adopted it.
Workshop cue: higher standards are not "extra credit" after compliance. They are the main way a community converts minimum NFIP eligibility rules into local resilience policy.
44 CFR 60.3(a) — Minimum Review Even Before FEMA Maps

60.3(a) is the baseline NFIP rule for a community that has indicated flood hazards but does not yet have FEMA-defined SFHAs, BFEs, floodways, or coastal high hazard areas. The point is not "no map, no rules"; the point is "use permits and available information to keep development reasonably safe."

TopicPractical meaning

Other permitsCheck that required federal or state permits are obtained, such as wetland or water-quality permits where applicable.
Reasonably safe from floodingIf the site is flood-prone, new construction and substantial improvements need anchoring, flood-damage-resistant materials, flood-minimizing methods, and protected/elevated utilities.
Subdivisions and new developmentReview layouts to minimize flood damage, provide adequate drainage, and keep public utilities/facilities out of avoidable flood damage.
Water systemsNew and replacement water supply systems should minimize or eliminate floodwater infiltration.
Sewer and onsite disposalSanitary sewer and onsite waste disposal systems should avoid floodwater infiltration, discharge into floodwaters, impairment, and contamination.
Exam cue: "reasonably safe" usually points to practical mitigation details such as anchoring tanks, elevating electrical/HVAC/service equipment, waterproofing vulnerable components, using flood-resistant materials, and installing backflow protection where sewage backup is a risk.
44 CFR 60.3(b)(1)-(8) — When Approximate Zone A Is Mapped

60.3(b) applies when FEMA has provided a FIRM or FHBM showing Zone A / SFHA, but not BFEs, floodway data, or coastal high hazard areas. It builds on 60.3(a) and adds Zone A permit, BFE-data, watercourse, and manufactured-home triggers.

ParagraphRuleExam cue

44 CFR 60.3(c) — BFE Exists, No Floodway Yet

60.3(c) applies when FEMA has provided final BFEs on the FIRM, but has not yet identified a regulatory floodway or coastal high hazard area. This is the regulatory bridge between the mapping workshop and the nonstructural mitigation workshop.

TopicRuleWorkshop cue
Residential elevationNew construction and substantial improvements in A1-30, AE, and AH zones must have the lowest floor, including basement, elevated to or above BFE.Elevation is not just mitigation best practice; once BFE exists, it is the basic residential compliance path.
Lowest floor meaningLowest floor includes the lowest enclosed area and basement, but excludes unfinished flood-resistant enclosures used only for parking, building access, or storage if they have compliant flood openings.Screen porches, converted storage, or utilities in the enclosure can create compliance problems.
Non-residential optionNon-residential structures may either elevate to or above BFE or be made watertight below BFE with components designed for hydrostatic, hydrodynamic, and buoyancy loads.This is why dry floodproofing is a commercial/non-residential tool, not a residential tool.
Floodproofing certificateA registered engineer or architect must certify accepted design/construction standards, and the community must keep the certification and floodproofing elevation record.Barrier hardware alone is not enough; certification, design height, O&M, and records matter.
Enclosures and openingsFully enclosed areas below the lowest floor must automatically equalize flood forces: at least two openings, total net area at least 1 in² per 1 ft² of enclosed area, bottom no more than 1 ft above grade, unless engineered/certified.This is the legal logic behind wet floodproofing: let water in to prevent wall collapse.
Manufactured homesManufactured homes placed or substantially improved outside a park, in a new or expanded park, or in a park where the home had flood-related substantial damage must be on a permanent foundation, elevated to or above BFE, and anchored against flotation, collapse, and lateral movement.This is a housing-equity issue as much as a technical rule: manufactured home parks can concentrate flood vulnerability.
Existing manufactured home parksReplacement homes in existing parks may have a lower federal minimum, but they still must be reasonably safe; many communities adopt a higher local standard such as BFE or BFE plus freeboard.Do not assume "existing park" means no elevation concern. Zone AO non-residentialNon-residential structures must either elevate above highest adjacent grade by the FIRM depth number, at least 2 ft if no depth is shown, or be floodproofed to that level including utility and sanitary facilities.This is the AO version of the non-residential "elevate or floodproof" choice.
Fill and encroachmentBefore a regulatory floodway is designated, development including fill in A1-30 and AE areas must not cumulatively raise the base-flood water-surface elevation more than 1 ft at any point.Fill can shift drainage, increase BFEs, increase velocity, cause erosion/scour, or weaken the structure built on it.

Recreational vehiclesRVs in SFHA must either be short-term/ready for highway use or meet the permit, elevation, and anchoring requirements for manufactured homes.RV status depends on mobility and temporary use; permanent attachments can make it non-ready for highway use.
Workshop relevance: 60.3(c) is directly tied to Workshop 2 because it controls elevation, non-residential dry floodproofing, wet-floodproofed enclosures, flood openings, and certification. It also ties back to Workshop 1 because BFE accuracy determines the compliance elevation.
44 CFR 60.3(d) — Regulatory Floodway and the No-Rise Rule

60.3(d) applies when FEMA has provided final BFEs and data from which the community must designate a regulatory floodway. Once the floodway is adopted, the rule becomes much stricter than the pre-floodway 1-ft surcharge concept: development in the floodway must show no increase in flood levels unless FEMA approves a map/floodway revision first.

TopicRulePractical cue
Carry forward 60.3(c)The community must still meet 60.3(c)(1)-(14), including elevation, floodproofing, openings, manufactured-home, AO/AH drainage, and RV requirements.60.3(d) adds floodway controls; it does not replace the BFE-based building standards.

44 CFR 60.3(e) — V/VE Coastal High Hazard Area Requirements

60.3(e) applies when coastal high hazard areas are identified on the FIRM as V1-30, VE, and/or V zones. The key shift is that wave action changes the compliance reference point: A zones focus on the lowest floor; V zones focus on the bottom of the lowest horizontal structural member.

Breakaway wallsBreakaway walls generally must have design safe loading resistance between 10 and 20 psf; stronger walls require registered engineer or architect certification that they will fail before base-flood loads damage the elevated building.The wall is sacrificial by design; it must not transfer destructive wave loads into the main structure.

Manufactured homesManufactured homes placed or substantially improved in V1-30, V, and VE zones generally must meet the V-zone standards in 60.3(e)(2)-(7), including elevation, open foundation, certification, no structural-support fill, and dune/mangrove protections.The same stricter treatment applies outside a park, in a new park/subdivision, in an expansion, or where flood-related substantial damage occurred in an existing park.

Workshop cue: dry floodproofing is not a V-zone solution. The minimum compliant posture is elevated, open, pile/column-supported construction that lets wave energy pass under the building.

Substantial Damage / Substantial Improvement

The 50% Rule — Critical Regulatory Trigger
  • Substantial Damage (SD): Cost to restore = ≥50% of structure's pre-damage fair market value → must bring to current code (elevate or acquire)
  • Substantial Improvement (SI): Any reconstruction/improvement where cost ≥50% of pre-improvement market value → same requirement
  • Cumulative substantial improvement: many ordinances track cumulative improvements over 10-year rolling period
  • The 50% threshold is NFIP minimum; communities can lower it (ASFPM recommends 30%)
  • After Aug 9-10, 2025 Milwaukee floods: many structures hit SD threshold → mandatory elevation or buyout
  • Common disputes: what counts toward the 50%? Code-required work? Unrelated renovations?
  • Repetitive Loss (RL): 2+ paid NFIP flood losses of more than $1,000 each in any 10-year period since 1978; Severe Repetitive Loss (SRL): 4+ claims over $5,000 each and over $20,000 cumulative, or 2+ building-only claims exceeding the structure's current value
IssueWorkshop-relevant cue
SI formulaCost of improvement or repair divided by market value before improvement or damage. If the result is ≥50%, the structure must meet current floodplain standards.
Market value basisUse the market value of the structure only, based on its condition before the improvement or repair. Exclude land value, site improvements, use/occupancy value, and location premium.
Valuation timingFor general improvements, use structure value before the start of construction. For deteriorated buildings, use value at permit application. For substantial damage, use pre-damage value.
When SD appliesSubstantial damage requirements apply when the repair cost is ≥50%, the building is in the SFHA, the community formally declares substantial damage, and the structure is not compliant with the current ordinance or regulations.
Pre-FIRM / Post-FIRMSD requirements apply to both Pre-FIRM and Post-FIRM structures. Pre-FIRM means built before the community's initial FIRM or on/before Dec. 31, 1974; Post-FIRM means built on/after the initial FIRM or after Dec. 31, 1974, whichever is later.
Substantial damage linkA substantially damaged structure is treated as substantially improved regardless of the actual repair work the owner proposes.
Costs to includeMaterials, labor, donated materials, volunteer or unpaid labor value, site preparation, demolition, compliance work, interior/exterior finishes, utilities/service equipment, roofing, siding, foundations, remodeling, porches, patios, and attached decks.
Cost sourcesUse itemized contractor or design-professional estimates, independent professional estimators, or professional cost data such as Marshall & Swift, RSMeans, or similar estimating tools. Be consistent across applicants.
Administrator roleMonitor permits and repairs, determine repair costs and market values, make SI/SD determinations, require current-code compliance as a permit condition, and require additional work if the applicant's proposal does not bring the structure into compliance.
Community responsibilityOnly the local community makes the official substantial damage determination. It should issue a determination letter as soon as possible, provide an appeal process, and keep repair-cost and market-value estimates accurate and consistent.
Practical cue: SI/SD is not only an insurance concept. It is the local permit trigger that converts an ordinary repair, addition, or rehabilitation into a bring-to-current-flood-standards decision.

Community Rating System (CRS)

CRS Structure and Critiques
  • Voluntary NFIP program; ~1,500 enrolled communities (of 22,700+)
  • Credit points → CRS class (1–10) → premium discount (5% per class, up to 45% for Class 1)
  • Activities: public information, mapping/regulation, flood damage reduction, warning/response
  • Documentation burden: universal complaint; smaller communities lack staff to document activities
  • Equity critique: Communities most needing relief (lower-income, less-capacity) least able to achieve high CRS class; administratively regressive
  • Class 1–4 communities: <50 nationally; most are Class 6–8 (15–25% discount)
  • CRS credit for freeboard: 2 ft above BFE = significant credit boost; this is why WI NR 116's BFE+2 standard is also a CRS strategy

Risk Rating 2.0

How Risk Rating 2.0 Works and Why It's Controversial
  • Implemented Oct 2021 (new policies) / Apr 2022 (renewals)
  • Pricing factors: Cost to rebuild (replacement cost value), distance to water body, foundation type, ground elevation relative to flooding sources, flood frequency
  • Key departure: no longer based solely on FIRM zone — property-specific risk replaces map-based pricing
  • Winners: properties in high-risk zones but physically elevated → often see decreases
  • Losers: properties in moderate-risk zones but physically close to water → often see increases
  • Policy count impact: 11–39% drop depending on state (largest drops in higher-risk coastal states)
  • Glide path: increases capped at 18%/year for existing policyholders
  • Louisiana lawsuit (E.D. La.): challenged RR2.0 as arbitrary/capricious under APA; survived preliminary injunction; ongoing
  • Congressional pressure: multiple bills to delay or modify RR2.0; none enacted as of 2026

NFIP — Current Status and Reauthorization

NFIP at 50th ASFPM: The Fiscal and Political Crisis
  • Created: August 1, 1968, through the National Flood Insurance Act of 1968; first policies issued in 1969
  • Original purpose: reduce federal disaster assistance expenditures, reduce flood risk through local floodplain regulation, and provide access to flood insurance
  • Currently: 4.6M policies, $1.3T coverage, 22,700+ communities
  • Treasury debt: $20B+ (Congress forgave $16B in 2017 post-Harvey/Irma/Maria)
  • Authorization expires: September 30, 2026 — the 35th short-term extension since FY2017
  • Structural deficit: Risk Rating 2.0 was designed to restore actuarial soundness but 11–39% policy drop reduces premium base
  • Legislative environment: comprehensive reform bills introduced every Congress since 2012 Biggert-Waters Act; none enacted except Biggert-Waters (2012) and HFIAA (2014)
  • Key reform proposals in circulation: means-tested means assistance, mandatory coverage for all federally-backed mortgages, private market competition, community-level policies
Conference angle: With NFIP expiring 3 months after this conference and FEMA under restructuring pressure, the reauthorization question is existential. Practitioners here will be living its consequences daily.
NFIP Three-Legged Stool — Know, Manage, Insure the Risk

The NFIP is often explained as a three-legged stool. The program only works when hazard identification, floodplain management, and insurance are all present.

Mandatory Purchase, Lender Determinations, and LOMA

When Flood Insurance Is Mandatory

Flood insurance availability is broad, but the federal mandatory purchase requirement is narrower. It is triggered by the combination of an insurable building or mobile home, an SFHA location, a participating NFIP community, and a covered loan or federal assistance pathway.

TriggerPractical ruleWorkshop cue
SFHA + federally regulated lenderFlood insurance is required for buildings or mobile homes securing loans from federally regulated, supervised, or insured lenders when the structure is in an SFHA.This is the ordinary mortgage trigger. The issue is not only the parcel boundary; the structure securing the loan matters.
Government-backed mortgageFHA, VA, USDA, and similar federal or federally backed mortgage programs apply mandatory flood-insurance rules when the covered structure is in the SFHA.Useful shorthand: if federal money or federal backing is in the mortgage and the building is in Zone A or V, expect mandatory purchase analysis.
MIRE eventsLenders must make or update flood determinations when a covered loan is made, increased, renewed, or extended.Owners can be surprised when a refinance, extension, or credit increase triggers a new flood determination on an old property.
Discovery during servicingIf a lender discovers that required flood insurance is missing or insufficient, force-placement procedures can be triggered after notice."Discovered" is a servicing/enforcement moment. It is not the same as FEMA changing the map.

Letting coverage lapse can affect later disaster aid eligibility.
Partial structure overlapIf a structure is partially or fully in the SFHA, the lender may treat the mandatory purchase requirement as triggered.Do not rely on "most of the lot is outside." The building footprint and the lender's determination drive the loan requirement.
Lender discretionA lender may require flood insurance even where federal law does not, including outside the SFHA or above the federal minimum amount.This is why a property can be outside mandatory purchase and still face a lender-imposed insurance requirement.
Workshop cue: the mandatory-purchase rule is a finance trigger, not a complete risk statement. It tells you when federal lending law requires insurance; it does not tell you whether the building is actually safe, whether pluvial risk exists, or whether insurance is wise outside the SFHA.

GFIP and Flood Insurance Maintenance After Disaster Assistance

The disaster-assistance slides matter because they explain a second insurance obligation, separate from the mortgage rule: after some federal disaster assistance, the owner must maintain flood insurance to remain eligible for future flood-disaster assistance.

ItemPractical meaningWorkshop cue
Who is affectedProperty owners who receive federal disaster assistance for acquisition, construction, repair, or replacement of an insurable flood-damaged structure in an SFHA.Connects IA, HMA, insurance, and rebuilding decisions after the event.

Coverage termGFIP coverage is temporary: the regulatory term is 36 months, beginning 60 days after the disaster declaration, with individual coverage effective on the 30th day after NFIP receives the required data and premium payment.This creates a transition clock that starts during recovery, when owners are least likely to track insurance deadlines.

Sequence of Delivery is the order used to route disaster survivors through available resources while preventing two programs from paying for the same loss. It is directly relevant to post-event nonstructural mitigation because insurance, FEMA IA, SBA loans, voluntary agencies, and HMA programs each have different timing and eligibility constraints.

FFRMS — Federal Flood Risk Management Standard

FFRMS: Rescinded, Regulatory Limbo
  • Originally established by Obama EO 13690 (Jan 2015); required federal agencies to use higher design flood standard
  • Three approaches: Freeboard Value (BFE+2/3 ft) / Climate-Informed Science (2080 projected flood) / Floodplain Value (500-yr)

Risk Rating 2.0 — Detailed Policy Analysis

RR2.0 Design, Impact, Legal Challenge

Argument Bank

Evidence-backed claims for discussion — cite numbers, not opinions

WS1 Geospatial Arguments

BLE Limitations
BLE is being used as a de facto regulatory tool without the validation it requires.
BLE BFEs can deviate 1–3 ft from detailed study BFEs in watersheds without surveyed channel bathymetry. Yet communities with BLE but no effective FIRM are making permitting decisions based on it. FEMA labels BLE "non-regulatory" but provides no guidance on permissible uses in local ordinance administration.
Datum Transition Risk
The NAVD88 → NAPGD2022 transition will require re-evaluation of virtually every elevation certificate and BFE in the country.
NAVD88 carries a ~0.5 m national bias and ~1 m coast-to-coast tilt. Properties certified as "above BFE" today may shift status after transformation — or vice versa. FEMA has not yet published a transition roadmap for the NFHL, FIRMs, or elevation certificates.
Mapping Coverage Gap
The NFHL's >90% population coverage statistic conceals massive accuracy heterogeneity.
A 1990 flood study in a rural county and a 2024 2D HEC-RAS study in an urban corridor are both "in the NFHL." The 90% number counts both. Meanwhile, pluvial flooding — the dominant urban risk — has essentially zero FIRM coverage anywhere in the U.S.
Calibration Standards
Single-event calibration is insufficient for flood mapping models used to set regulatory BFEs.
Professional hydraulic modeling practice (HEC-RAS guidance, FEMA MH+T) recommends ≥2 independent events for calibration. Many FEMA-funded models are calibrated to one observed event, sometimes with limited observed WSE data, and then used to set BFEs that affect property values and insurance rates for decades.

WS2 Nonstructural Arguments

Residential Dry Floodproofing Prohibition
The blanket prohibition on residential dry floodproofing is a regulatory artifact that hasn't kept pace with barrier technology or pluvial flood risk realities.
44 CFR 60.3 was written when "floodproofing" meant masonry wall sealing — leak-prone and failure-prone. ANSI/FM 2510 (2020) establishes performance standards for deployable barriers to 3+ ft. No rulemaking to revisit the residential prohibition is pending. Meanwhile, pluvial urban flooding — not regulated by FIRMs — is the fastest-growing urban risk, leaving homeowners with no compliant active mitigation option.
Buyout Equity
HMGP/FMA voluntary buyouts, as implemented, systematically disadvantage renters and low-income homeowners.
Renters receive no compensation under current buyout programs; only property owners are paid. Pre-flood fair market value in flood-prone neighborhoods is often below replacement cost of comparable housing, because flood risk is already discounted into property prices. "Successful" buyouts may simply relocate flood vulnerability to other affordable-but-flood-prone areas.
BCA Discount Rate
FEMA's 7% BCA discount rate systematically under-values long-lived mitigation investments, making cost-effective projects appear infeasible.
At 7%, a $1M benefit in year 30 is worth $131K today. At 3% (UK HM Treasury standard for long-lived infrastructure), it's $412K. The choice of discount rate is a policy decision — not an actuarial truth. Many BRIC-eligible community projects that would pass a 3% BCR test fail at 7%. The NIBS $7:1 median BCR for riverine flood mitigation was calculated at the 7% rate — meaning the true societal return is higher.
CRS Equity
The CRS systematically rewards communities with administrative capacity, not necessarily those with the greatest flood risk or mitigation need.
~1,500 of 22,700+ NFIP communities participate in CRS. Class 1–4 (35–45% discount) = <50 communities. The documentation burden required for high CRS class is feasible only for well-staffed, well-funded planning departments — typically larger, wealthier communities. Smaller, lower-income, higher-risk communities that most need premium relief are least equipped to achieve meaningful CRS class.
Pluvial Flooding Gap
Pluvial flooding is the fastest-growing urban flood risk and is essentially unmapped by the current FIRM system.
~25% of NFIP claims come from outside the SFHA — many attributable to pluvial flooding. Urban catchment concentration times under 30 minutes. Storm sewer systems designed for 2–10 year storms are overwhelmed by 100-year events. FEMA has no systematic methodology for pluvial FIRM delineation. Milwaukee August 9–10, 2025 was primarily pluvial — most damaged properties were not in the SFHA.
Mandatory Purchase vs. Real Risk
The federal mandatory purchase requirement is a lending trigger, not a complete flood-risk signal.
Mandatory purchase generally follows SFHA status plus a covered loan or federal assistance pathway. A LOMA can remove the federal purchase trigger when natural ground supports removal, but it does not mean the site is risk-free and may not directly reduce a Risk Rating 2.0 premium. Conversely, lenders may require flood insurance even where federal law does not. This is why map status, lender determinations, insurance pricing, and physical risk must be discussed separately.
Recovery Funding Sequence
Nonstructural mitigation fails when the recovery funding sequence is treated as an administrative afterthought.
Insurance can pay covered flood claims without a presidential declaration; FEMA IA depends on a declared IA area and cannot duplicate insurance; SBA loans may fill gaps but must be repaid; HMA funding arrives on a different timeline. If communities do not explain Sequence of Delivery early, owners may spend claim proceeds on like-for-like repairs before elevation, relocation, acquisition, ICC, or lawful floodproofing options are evaluated.
ICC as Implementation Test
ICC is the practical test of whether a community can connect insurance, permits, SI/SD, and mitigation fast enough after a flood.
ICC may provide up to $30,000 for elevation, relocation, demolition, or eligible floodproofing, but only after a qualifying local substantial-damage or repetitive-damage determination and a separate claim process. If the community lacks pre-event SD procedures, owner messaging, permit triage, and contractor-estimate workflows, the owner may exhaust ordinary claim proceeds before the mitigation pathway becomes usable.
GFIP Transition Risk
GFIP solves an immediate uninsured-survivor problem but can become a future-assistance trap if the transition to standard flood insurance is not managed.
GFIP is temporary and limited. It can satisfy the initial flood-insurance requirement after IHP assistance, but the survivor must maintain required coverage after it expires. If communities, states, FEMA, NFIP Direct, and agents do not coordinate outreach before the 36-month term ends, a survivor may lose coverage and jeopardize future federal disaster assistance.
USACE Delivery Capacity
Nonstructural mitigation is becoming a federal delivery-capacity question, not just a mitigation-method question.
USACE has had statutory nonstructural authority since WRDA Section 73 in 1974 and built floodproofing expertise through the National Nonstructural Committee, ERDC testing, Silver Jackets, and major projects. ASFPM reports 88,000+ structures identified for nonstructural mitigation in approved or developing USACE projects. FIMP alone targets 4,000+ structures in the 10-year floodplain, with a 245-structure pilot. The contested question is whether USACE will embrace or pause this implementation role while FEMA mitigation remains unstable.

Strategic Foresight

Five scenarios shaping floodplain management 2026–2035

Scenario 1
NFIP Reform: Comprehensive Reauthorization (Low Probability, High Impact)

Congress passes NFIP reform bill before September 30, 2026 expiry: means-tested assistance, mandatory coverage expansion, private market competition framework, and FFRD as future regulatory baseline.

Implications: Risk Rating 2.0 modified or frozen. Private market growth. FIRM replacement with probabilistic products accelerated. CRS reformed to reduce documentation burden. FEMA mapping budget potentially increased.

Conference angle: Ask presenters: "If comprehensive reform passes, what's the first thing you'd change about mapping methodology?"

Scenario 2
NFIP Muddles Through: Another Short-Term Extension (High Probability)

Congress passes 36th short-term extension; no comprehensive reform; Risk Rating 2.0 continues; policy count continues declining; fiscal deficit grows. Status quo for floodplain mapping, BRIC litigation continues.

Implications: Community frustration deepens. Practitioners at this conference will be managing the same structural tensions in 2027. Private flood insurance fills some gap. Federal disaster relief becomes de facto substitute for NFIP.

Conference angle: "Given this is almost certainly scenario, how are communities building resilience independent of federal program stability?"

Scenario 3
FEMA Review Council Restructuring Enacted (Medium Probability)

RAPID, R3P, FAIR programs enacted legislatively. Parametric block grants replace project-by-project PA. HMGP replaced by two-phase R3P. Disaster declaration thresholds raised.

Implications: Fewer presidential disaster declarations. States absorb more cost. Communities that can't document damage quantitatively get lower RAPID grants. Floodplain management programs may be decoupled from disaster assistance more cleanly.

Conference angle: Milwaukee's PA denial was under the current system. Under RAPID, would Milwaukee have gotten more or less? The parametric formula matters enormously.

Scenario 4
Datum Transition Disruption (Certain, Timeline Uncertain)

NAPGD2022 is adopted mid-2026 (FGCS vote). NAVD88-based products begin mandatory transition. Elevation certificates, FIRMs, BFEs all need transformation.

Implications: Properties near BFE with NAVD88 ECs may gain or lose compliance status. Litigation potential when property owners find their "above BFE" status changes. FEMA needs 5–10 year transformation plan. Surveying community has both a crisis and an opportunity.

Conference angle: "Is FEMA planning to grandfather existing ECs, or will every EC need to be retranscribed to NAPGD2022 upon adoption?"

Scenario 5
Pluvial Flood Mapping Initiative (Aspirational, 5–10 years)

Congress or FEMA launches systematic urban pluvial flood mapping program — analogous to Risk MAP but for stormwater/pluvial risk. Probabilistic depth grids replace SFHA delineation for urban areas.

Implications: Fundamentally transforms what "flood zone" means for urban homeowners. ~25% of current "outside SFHA" claims would shift to insurable zones. Would require NFIP reform (mandatory purchase in pluvial zones?). MMSD's Fresh Coast 740 becomes a model.

Conference angle: Milwaukee's August 2025 flood — mostly pluvial, mostly outside SFHA — is the strongest possible argument for this initiative. Make this argument explicitly.

NFIP AvailabilityFlood insurance is generally available in participating NFIP communities inside and outside the SFHA, but not in nonparticipating or suspended communities, for some CBRS/OPA post-designation structures, or for Section 1316 properties
NFIP Coverage LimitsMaximum policy amounts; in the Regular Program, common residential building coverage is capped at $250,000 with $100,000 contents coverage, while many non-residential policies can reach $500,000 building and $500,000 contents
NIBSNational Institute of Building Sciences; published 2017 study showing $7:1 BCR for natural hazard mitigation
NNCUSACE National Nonstructural Committee; successor to the National Nonstructural / Flood Proofing Committee, focused on nonstructural flood risk management expertise
NR 116Wisconsin administrative code governing floodplain management; FPE = BFE+2 ft; 0.01-ft floodway tolerance
NVANon-Vegetated Vertical Accuracy; lidar accuracy measured on hard surfaces; primary accuracy metric; RMSEz-based
OMRRROperation, Maintenance, Repair, Replacement, and Rehabilitation; post-construction responsibility question in USACE and local sponsor projects
OMB Circular A-94Office of Management and Budget guidance on discount rates for federal programs; sets 7% real discount rate for BCA
ONAOther Needs Assistance; FEMA Individual Assistance category for eligible disaster-related needs beyond housing, with post-March 22, 2024 reforms removing the old SBA-loan gate for certain categories
OPAOtherwise Protected Area; protected coastal barrier area related to CBRS where federal flood insurance restrictions may apply to post-designation construction or substantial improvement
PAPublic Assistance; FEMA reimbursement program for local governments' disaster costs; proposed replacement: RAPID
PEDPreconstruction Engineering and Design; USACE phase between feasibility and construction where designs, agreements, and implementation details are developed
Pluvial FloodingFlooding driven by rainfall exceeding soil/stormwater capacity; not riverine; dominates urban areas; essentially unmapped by current FIRM system
PMRPhysical Map Revision; full FIRM/FIS revision process for updated flood hazard data, usually with public review and formal community adoption
Primary ResidencePolicyholder's principal residence; relevant to some NFIP surcharges, discounts, and replacement-cost loss settlement treatment
PPAProject Partnership Agreement; formal agreement between USACE and a Non-federal Sponsor defining cost share, responsibilities, and project implementation terms
QL0/QL1/QL2/QL3USGS 3DEP lidar quality levels; QL2 = minimum for FEMA mapping (≥2 pts/m², ≤10 cm RMSEz); QL1 preferred for floodplain mapping
R3PResilience, Recovery, and Rebuild Program; proposed FEMA Review Council replacement for HMGP; two-phase structure
RAPIDResponsive Aid for Public Infrastructure Disaster; proposed FEMA Review Council replacement for Public Assistance; parametric block grant
Rating FactorsVariables used in flood insurance pricing, including geographic location, distance to flooding source, relative elevation, structural variables, coverage choices, claims history, and discounts
RCVReplacement Cost Value; cost to replace damaged property with the same kind of material and construction without deducting depreciation; also a Risk Rating 2.0 pricing variable
Regular ProgramNFIP community status with full flood insurance coverage limits and ongoing floodplain management requirements, distinct from the lower-limit Emergency Program
Repetitive Loss (RL)NFIP-insured structure with ≥2 paid flood losses of more than $1,000 each in any 10-year period since 1978; definitions can differ by insurance, grant, CRS, ICC, and local ordinance context
Repetitive Loss Higher StandardLocal ordinance option that requires repetitive-loss structures to meet substantial-damage-style current-code compliance even when a single event does not independently trigger the NFIP minimum 50% SD rule
Recreational VehicleIn the SFHA, an RV must meet manufactured-home elevation and anchoring requirements unless it is on site fewer than 180 consecutive days or is fully licensed and ready for highway use
Restrictive CovenantRecorded property restriction used in nonstructural projects to preserve mitigation conditions and bind future owners
Remote SensingData collection from aircraft, satellites, drones, or sensors; in flood mapping, LiDAR and imagery can support terrain, land cover, change detection, and flood documentation, but satellite imagery alone does not set regulatory BFEs
Risk MAPRisk Mapping, Assessment, and Planning; FEMA's flood mapping program; produces FIRMs and risk products
Risk Rating 2.0NFIP pricing methodology implemented Oct 2021; property-specific pricing replacing FIRM zone-based rates; associated with 11–39% policy declines in some states
RMSEzRoot Mean Square Error in the vertical dimension; primary accuracy metric for lidar in ASPRS 2024
ROERight of Entry; property-owner authorization allowing project officials or contractors to enter private property for surveys, inspections, or construction-related work
SBA Disaster LoanLow-interest federal disaster loan for homeowners, renters, businesses, and many private nonprofits; approved loans need not be accepted, but received loan funds must be repaid
SDESubstantial Damage Estimator; FEMA tool communities can use to estimate building damage consistently and support substantial damage determinations
SDPSubstantial Damage Management Plan; CRS Activity 510 element for pre-event substantial damage planning, SDE pre-population, and pre-event mitigation alternatives
SEASupplemental Environmental Assessment; NEPA environmental review document supplementing prior analysis when project details or impacts need additional review
Section 1316National Flood Insurance Act enforcement tool allowing flood insurance denial for a property declared by the community or state to be in violation of floodplain management regulations; it does not put the property into the NFIP Emergency Program
Sequence of DeliveryOrder for routing disaster survivors through emergency aid, insurance, FEMA IA/ONA, SBA loans, other programs, and unmet-needs resources while preventing duplication of benefits
SFHASpecial Flood Hazard Area; area with 1% or greater annual chance of flooding; regulatory zone for NFIP
SISubstantial Improvement; reconstruction, rehabilitation, addition, or other improvement where cost ≥50% of the structure's market value before the start of construction; substantially damaged structures are treated as SI regardless of actual repair work
Silver JacketsUSACE-led interagency teams that coordinate federal, state, and local flood risk management and often support nonstructural approaches
SLOSHSea, Lake, and Overland Surges from Hurricanes; NOAA storm surge model; used in coastal FIRM production
SRLSevere Repetitive Loss; NFIP-insured structure with ≥4 claim payments over $5,000 each and over $20,000 cumulative, or ≥2 building-only claim payments whose cumulative amount exceeds the current structure value, with at least two payments within 10 years
STORM ActSafeguarding Tomorrow through Ongoing Risk Mitigation Act; IIJA-funded revolving loan fund; $500M FY22–26
StreamStatsUSGS web application for estimating peak flows at ungauged sites using regression equations
Temporary OccupancyLocal post-disaster permission, if allowed, for limited use of a damaged structure; should be documented separately from permanent repair approval and SI/SD compliance
Three-Legged StoolNFIP framing: know the risk through hazard identification and maps, manage the risk through regulations and mitigation, and insure the risk through flood insurance
Substantial Damage (SD)Damage where cost to restore ≥50% of pre-damage fair market value; triggers bring-to-code (elevation or acquisition) requirement
Substantial Damage SurveyField and administrative assessment process used after damage to estimate whether repair costs equal or exceed 50% of the structure's pre-damage market value
Swift CurrentFEMA HMA program; $500M for streamlined post-disaster buyouts of SRL/RL properties
3DEPUSGS 3D Elevation Program; national lidar acquisition initiative; goal: QL2 coverage of entire U.S.
USACEU.S. Army Corps of Engineers; federal water resources agency with flood risk management, coastal storm risk management, and nonstructural mitigation authorities
V Zone / VE ZoneCoastal high hazard area; subject to wave action; dry floodproofing absolutely prohibited; elevated foundations required
VVAVegetated Vertical Accuracy; lidar accuracy under forest canopy; always worse than NVA; reported at 95th percentile
Waiting PeriodDelay before new NFIP coverage or increased coverage becomes effective; most policies have a 30-day wait, with limited exceptions such as qualifying loan events, map changes, and post-wildfire flooding
Wet FloodproofingAllow water to enter; use flood-resistant materials; required for enclosed areas below BFE in residential structures; flood openings required
WRDAWater Resources Development Act; recurring federal legislation authorizing USACE water resources projects and policy changes, including nonstructural flood mitigation authorities
WSEWater Surface Elevation; the elevation of the water surface at a given location and time; used for calibration and BFE determination

X Zone (unshaded)Minimal flood hazard; outside 500-yr floodplain; lowest regulatory burden
