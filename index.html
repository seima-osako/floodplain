Workshop 1: Geospatial Decision Making

"They did what??? Geospatial decision making, How it drives the data that applies to floodplain management and mapping" — AECOM (Morgan, Davis, Peterson)

Core Argument of This Workshop

Every regulatory outcome—BFE, flood zone, LOMC, insurance rate—traces back to geospatial decisions made years or decades ago, often with tools and data far inferior to today's. Practitioners who understand those choices can challenge, improve, and communicate them. Those who don't are at the mercy of legacy data.

AECOM Presenter Context

  • Hope Morgan: NCPLS (NC Professional Land Surveyor) + GISP + CFM — brings geodetic accuracy, datum expertise, legal boundary surveying perspective to GIS
  • Scott Davis: NC WAM + NC SAM + SWIT — wetland/stream assessment; field-calibration mindset
  • Kelsey Peterson: CFM — floodplain management implementation, community compliance
  • Expect NC case studies; AECOM does large-scale risk MAP work

Flood Map Fundamentals

FIRM — Flood Insurance Rate Map
  • Regulatory map used by communities for permitting under 44 CFR 60.3
  • Zones: AE = detailed study with BFE; A = approximate, no BFE; AO = shallow sheet flow (depth shown); AH = shallow ponding with BFE; V/VE = coastal wave action; X-shaded = 500-yr; X-unshaded = minimal hazard
  • FIRM panels updated piecemeal — not a unified national snapshot
  • Effective date can be 5–30+ years old for rural areas
  • FIS (Flood Insurance Study) = engineering report that underlies the FIRM; contains the hydraulic model, frequency analysis, cross-sections, and BFE profiles
  • FIRM = regulatory product; BLE = non-regulatory screening tool (unless state converts it)
NFHL — National Flood Hazard Layer
  • FEMA's GIS database aggregating all effective FIRM data nationwide
  • Covers >90% of U.S. population
  • Updated continuously with each new effective FIRM and each LOMC
  • NOT a unified snapshot — every polygon references its own effective date and study method
  • Critical limitation: querying NFHL gives you the current regulatory zone, not the current physical risk
  • Accessible via FEMA's National Flood Map Service Center and ArcGIS REST services

ASPRS Positional Accuracy Standards Ed. 2 V2 (2024)
  • Published 2024; supersedes 2014 edition
  • Key change 1: Primary metric is now RMSEz — 95% confidence interval factor eliminated as primary metric
  • Key change 2: Minimum checkpoints increased from 20 to 30
  • Key change 3: Checkpoint distribution rules tightened (vegetated vs. open terrain)
  • Vertical Accuracy Classes: RMSEz ≤5 cm, 10 cm, 20 cm, etc.
  • Non-Vegetated Vertical Accuracy (NVA) = hard surface checkpoints
  • Vegetated Vertical Accuracy (VVA) = under canopy — always worse than NVA; reported at 95th percentile
  • Why it matters: FEMA's lidar acceptance spec references ASPRS standards; a 2014-spec survey delivered in 2025 may not meet the updated standard

Base Level Engineering (BLE)

BLE — What It Is and What It Isn't
  • Automated 1D/2D HEC-RAS modeling at HUC-8 or HUC-12 scale using QL2 lidar terrain
  • Produced by FEMA contractors for screening/prioritization — NOT regulatory
  • Key limitation: no surveyed channel bathymetry → BFEs can be off by 1–3 ft
  • No field calibration required → model not validated against observed events
  • Some states have converted BLE to regulatory: Kansas KDA has; most have not
  • BLE is the starting point for community outreach — don't let it become the endpoint
  • BLE shows approximate floodplain extent; communities should commission detailed studies for regulated areas
Critical framing: BLE being shown as a "preliminary" map to communities who then assume it IS the regulatory map is a documented source of planning confusion. Raise this in discussion.

2D Modeling Pitfalls (Know These Cold)
  1. Mesh resolution: Too coarse = overestimates flood extent; too fine = computational instability. Rule of thumb: mesh cells ≤ 1/3 channel width
  2. Boundary conditions: Upstream flow hydrograph + downstream stage or normal depth. Wrong downstream BC = wrong backwater profile
  3. Terrain preprocessing: Must "burn" channel bathymetry into lidar DEM; must remove bridges/culverts from terrain (then re-add as structures)
  4. Manning's n calibration: Land cover-based n values without field calibration are educated guesses; ±20% uncertainty in peak BFE
  5. Bridge/culvert representation: HEC-RAS bridge routines work in 1D; in 2D you must use SA/2D connection or inline structures
  6. Calibration requirement: At least 2 independent flood events with observed WSEs and discharges; single-event calibration is insufficient
  7. Unsteady vs. steady: Unsteady better for dynamic floodplain; steady simpler but misses timing-related effects

Datum Transition: NAVD88 → NAPGD2022

Why This Matters to Every FIRM in America
  • NAVD88 (North American Vertical Datum of 1988) = current national vertical reference for all FIRMs, BFEs, elevation certificates
  • Known errors: biased ~0.5 m nationally; coast-to-coast tilt of ~1 m (higher in east, lower in west)
  • NAPGD2022 (North American-Pacific Geodetic Datum of 2022) = replacement; FGCS vote expected mid-2026
  • Transition will NOT happen overnight — FEMA, USGS, NOAA all need to update products
  • Implication: Every FIRM, BFE, and elevation certificate referencing NAVD88 will eventually need datum transformation; properties currently "above BFE" may shift status
  • Practical planning: communities near coasts where NAVD88 error is largest should begin assessing impact now
  • Elevation certificates should note datum; surveyors doing ECs now should be aware of future transformation requirement
The FGCS vote expected mid-2026 means this topic is live at the conference. Expect it to come up in workshop discussion.

Future of Flood Risk Data (FFRD)

FFRD — Probabilistic Risk Products
  • Joint USACE-FEMA Interagency Research Working Agreement (IRWA)
  • Goal: replace binary SFHA/non-SFHA with probabilistic, graduated risk products
  • Return period range: ~5-year to 2,000-year
  • Method: Monte Carlo via HEC-WAT (Watershed Analysis Tool) + ensemble hydraulic modeling
  • Three pilot watersheds currently underway (locations vary; check FEMA 2026 updates)
  • Non-regulatory at this time — not replacing FIRMs yet
  • If implemented: could transform NFIP pricing, CRS, and building code triggers
  • Biggest challenge: regulatory framework is binary (SFHA/non-SFHA); probabilistic products require legislative or rulemaking change

GIS / Geospatial Decision Making

How GIS Decisions Propagate Downstream

Every GIS decision—coordinate system, projection, datum, raster resolution, vectorization method, attribute schema—cascades into regulatory outcomes:

  1. Projection errors: Using a geographic CRS instead of projected CRS in HEC-RAS introduces systematic distance errors; cross-sections may be misaligned
  2. DEM resolution: 1m DEM vs. 3m DEM yields measurably different floodplain extents; FEMA requires ≤3m for detailed studies
  3. Datum confusion: Mixing NAVD88 and NGVD29 terrains without transformation is a documented source of BFE errors (can be 1-3 ft)
  4. Hydrologic connectivity: Sinks, flat areas, and bridges must be handled correctly in terrain preprocessing; automated fill-sink routines sometimes create phantom drainage paths
  5. Attribution errors: NFHL polygon attributes (zone designation, BFE, effective date) can be inconsistent across county boundaries due to stitching of different effective FIRMs
Workshop 1 key insight to share: The gap between QL2 lidar accuracy (10 cm RMSEz) and the 1-foot freeboard in most ordinances suggests communities are managing to a false precision. When BLE BFEs can be off by 1–3 ft due to missing bathymetry, the 1-foot surcharge standard is illusory. Push for mandatory bathymetric surveys in detailed study watersheds.

Workshop 2: Nonstructural Mitigation

"Nonstructural Mitigation for Pluvial, Fluvial, and Coastal Flood Events" — Behm, Little, Scott, Elvir

Core Argument of This Workshop

Structural measures (levees, floodwalls) are expensive, often inequitable, and create residual risk. Nonstructural approaches—elevation, relocation, buyouts, floodproofing, early warning—offer flexible, scalable, property-specific solutions that reduce both risk and federal fiscal exposure. The challenge is matching the right tool to each flood mechanism.

The Unresolved Tension (Know This)

NFIP PROHIBITS dry floodproofing of residential structures in the SFHA. (44 CFR 60.3)

Dry floodproofing of non-residential buildings in A zones = allowed. Dry floodproofing in V zones = NEVER (wave action would destroy seals).

No rulemaking to change the residential prohibition is pending. This is the most contested topic in Workshop 2's universe. Presenters may propose policy changes — be ready to probe the evidence base.

Nonstructural Mitigation Toolbox

Elevation (Freeboard / Raise Lowest Floor)
  • Most reliable long-term mitigation; reduces damage geometrically with elevation above BFE
  • NFIP rate reductions: 1 ft above BFE = ~30% premium reduction; 2 ft = ~60%; 3 ft = ~70%+
  • Practical limit: foundation type (slab, crawlspace, basement) determines cost and feasibility
  • Wisconsin NR 116: FPE = BFE + 2 ft (state freeboard = most stringent in Midwest)
  • FEMA Hazard Mitigation Grant Program funds elevation for SRL/RL properties
  • Acquisition preferred over elevation for properties ≥50% damaged (Substantial Damage trigger)
Relocation & Acquisition / Buyouts
  • Relocation: Move structure out of floodplain; preserves building; expensive; requires suitable alternate site
  • Acquisition: Purchase property at pre-flood fair market value; demolish; deed-restrict as open space; most permanent solution
  • FEMA requires acquired parcels remain open space in perpetuity (44 CFR 80)
  • Voluntary only — no eminent domain under HMGP/FMA
  • Key equity issue: buyout programs can displace low-income renters without compensating them; owners paid but renters lose housing
  • Swift Current program ($500M): expedited buyouts for SRL/RL properties post-disaster
  • Community resistance: "shrinkage" fears, tax base loss, neighborhood dissolution
Dry Floodproofing — Rules, Limits, ANSI/FM 2510

Definition: Make the building watertight below the DFE using barriers, sealed walls, waterproof coatings, check valves. Water does NOT enter.

  • Allowed: Non-residential structures in A zones (AE, A, AO, AH) — 44 CFR 60.3(c)(3)
  • NOT allowed: Residential structures in SFHA — 44 CFR 60.3 (no exception; no rulemaking pending)
  • NEVER: V zones (coastal wave action would destroy any seal)
  • Structural limit: FEMA P-936 limits application to ≤3 ft of flooding above BFE for most structures
  • Hydrostatic pressure at 3 ft water depth = 187 lb/ft²; at 6 ft = 375 lb/ft²
  • ANSI/FM 2510 (2020): Only U.S. national consensus standard for flood mitigation equipment — covers perimeter barriers, opening barriers, backwater valves, flood glazing
  • FEMA deployable barrier requirement in ASCE 24-24: written O&M plan + annual deployment drills + ANSI/FM 2510 compliance
The residential prohibition is the elephant in the room. Presenters from US Flood Control (Elvir) sell commercial flood barriers — watch for implicit arguments that the residential prohibition should be relaxed. Push back with: what is the failure rate data? What happens when barriers fail and residents stayed?

Wet Floodproofing

Definition: Allow water to enter; use flood-resistant materials and design to minimize damage. Water DOES enter.

  • Required for enclosed areas below BFE in residential structures (with flood openings)
  • Typically used for: crawlspaces, attached garages, below-grade parking, accessory structures
  • Flood openings (vents): must provide 1 sq in net open area per sq ft of enclosed space (NFIP standard) OR engineered alternative
  • FEMA Technical Bulletin 1 (2021): flood opening sizing requirements
  • FEMA Technical Bulletin 7 (2022): wet floodproofing design
  • Flood-resistant materials: FEMA TB-2 (2025 revision): Class 4 and 5 materials; concrete, CMU, ceramic tile, pressure-treated wood, closed-cell foam — can be submerged 72 hrs without significant damage
  • Does NOT satisfy NFIP minimum compliance for the main structure — only for ancillary spaces
Early Warning Systems (EWS)
  • Sensor network (rain gauges, stream gauges, soil moisture) + alert dissemination + community response protocol
  • USGS StreamStats / National Water Information System: real-time gauge data
  • NOAA Advanced Hydrologic Prediction Service (AHPS): 1-7 day forecasts at 4,000+ gauge locations
  • Texas Hill Country July 4, 2025: 139+ deaths despite gauge network — shows EWS alone insufficient without evacuation plan + public action
  • Key components: (1) detection, (2) forecast, (3) alert dissemination, (4) community action protocol
  • Equity concern: alerts in English only exclude non-English speakers; cell towers may fail in same storm
  • Integration with dry floodproofing: ANSI/FM 2510 / ASCE 24-24 require O&M plans including deployment trigger (flood warning stage)
  • FEMA BRIC funded many EWS projects before termination; status of funded projects unclear
Flood Openings — Technical Requirements
  • Required in all enclosed areas below BFE for NFIP compliance (residential)
  • Standard: 1 in² net open area per 1 ft² of enclosed floor area; minimum 2 openings; openings within 1 ft of grade
  • Engineered openings: must be certified by PE; can use smaller area if designed to equalize pressure faster
  • Automatic openings (ICC/ANSI 1209): rated to open at ≤12 in of water depth; close when water recedes; do not require manual operation
  • Covers: must be removable or automatically open; screen mesh counts against net area if flow restricted
  • FEMA TB-1 (2021) is the authoritative reference

ASCE 24-24 — New Building Standard

Four Key Changes from ASCE 24-14 to ASCE 24-24
  1. Flood Design Class (FDC) replaces simple occupancy categories:
    • FDC 1: Low-risk (ag storage, temporary)
    • FDC 2: Normal (typical residential, commercial)
    • FDC 3: High-risk (schools, fire stations, large assembly)
    • FDC 4: Essential facilities (hospitals, EOCs, critical infrastructure)
  2. Design Flood Elevation (DFE) now FDC-dependent:
    • FDC 1/2: 1% annual chance flood (100-yr) — same as before
    • FDC 3: 750-year MRI (0.13% annual chance)
    • FDC 4: 1,000-year MRI (0.1% annual chance)
  3. 500-year flood hazard area applies to FDC 2/3/4 buildings (expanded from 100-yr SFHA baseline for some purposes)
  4. Deployable barriers (temporary barriers): If used, must comply with ANSI/FM 2510; written O&M plan required; annual deployment drills required
ASCE 24-24 is NOT yet referenced in the 2024 International Building Code / International Residential Code. Target adoption: 2027 I-Code cycle. Until then, it is a best-practice reference, not a code requirement — unless local jurisdiction has adopted it independently.

Benefit-Cost Analysis (BCA)

FEMA BCA Toolkit & Key Numbers
  • Discount rate: 7% (OMB Circular A-94) — this is the rate at which future benefits are discounted to present value
  • Median riverine flood mitigation BCR: $7:1 (NIBS 2017 study; often cited as $6 per $1 invested)
  • HMGP and BRIC require BCR ≥ 1.0 for eligibility; FMA projects often need BCR ≥ 1.5 in practice
  • BCA benefit categories: avoided property damage, displacement costs (temporary housing), loss of function, mental stress / fatality risk, ecosystem services
  • Social equity benefits: removed by current administration; previously counted for BRIC
  • Critical BCA sensitivity: discount rate. At 7%, a future $1M benefit in year 30 is worth only $131K today. At 3%, it's $412K. Choice of rate is a political decision disguised as an accounting one.
  • BCA for elevation: straightforward using depth-damage functions. BCA for EWS: harder — must estimate lives saved and displacement avoided.

Floodway vs. Flood Fringe

  • Floodway: Channel + adjacent area needed to convey 100-yr flood without increasing BFE more than 1.00 ft (federal) or 0.01 ft (Wisconsin NR 116)
  • Flood fringe / floodway fringe: Between floodway and outer SFHA boundary; fill allowed if BFE not exceeded
  • Wisconsin's 0.01-ft tolerance is effectively a no-fill standard — most stringent in nation
  • Floodway designation requires encroachment analysis; errors in cross-section placement propagate to floodway width

Substantial Damage / Substantial Improvement

The 50% Rule — Critical Regulatory Trigger
  • Substantial Damage (SD): Cost to restore = ≥50% of structure's pre-damage fair market value → must bring to current code (elevate or acquire)
  • Substantial Improvement (SI): Any reconstruction/improvement where cost ≥50% of pre-improvement market value → same requirement
  • Cumulative substantial improvement: many ordinances track cumulative improvements over 10-year rolling period
  • The 50% threshold is NFIP minimum; communities can lower it (ASFPM recommends 30%)
  • After Aug 9-10, 2025 Milwaukee floods: many structures hit SD threshold → mandatory elevation or buyout
  • Common disputes: what counts toward the 50%? Code-required work? Unrelated renovations?
  • Repetitive Loss (RL): ≥2 paid claims ≥$1,000 over 10 years; Severe Repetitive Loss (SRL): ≥4 claims OR 2 claims exceeding building value

Community Rating System (CRS)

CRS Structure and Critiques
  • Voluntary NFIP program; ~1,500 enrolled communities (of 22,700+)
  • Credit points → CRS class (1–10) → premium discount (5% per class, up to 45% for Class 1)
  • Activities: public information, mapping/regulation, flood damage reduction, warning/response
  • Documentation burden: universal complaint; smaller communities lack staff to document activities
  • Equity critique: Communities most needing relief (lower-income, less-capacity) least able to achieve high CRS class; administratively regressive
  • Class 1–4 communities: <50 nationally; most are Class 6–8 (15–25% discount)
  • CRS credit for freeboard: 2 ft above BFE = significant credit boost; this is why WI NR 116's BFE+2 standard is also a CRS strategy

Risk Rating 2.0

How Risk Rating 2.0 Works and Why It's Controversial
  • Implemented Oct 2021 (new policies) / Apr 2022 (renewals)
  • Pricing factors: Cost to rebuild (replacement cost value), distance to water body, foundation type, ground elevation relative to flooding sources, flood frequency
  • Key departure: no longer based solely on FIRM zone — property-specific risk replaces map-based pricing
  • Winners: properties in high-risk zones but physically elevated → often see decreases
  • Losers: properties in moderate-risk zones but physically close to water → often see increases
  • Policy count impact: 11–39% drop depending on state (largest drops in higher-risk coastal states)
  • Glide path: increases capped at 18%/year for existing policyholders
  • Louisiana lawsuit (E.D. La.): challenged RR2.0 as arbitrary/capricious under APA; survived preliminary injunction; ongoing
  • Congressional pressure: multiple bills to delay or modify RR2.0; none enacted as of 2026

FFRMS — Federal Flood Risk Management Standard

FFRMS: Rescinded, Regulatory Limbo
  • Originally established by Obama EO 13690 (Jan 2015); required federal agencies to use higher design flood standard
  • Three approaches: Freeboard Value (BFE+2/3 ft) / Climate-Informed Science (2080 projected flood) / Floodplain Value (500-yr)
  • Rescinded by Trump EO 14148 (Jan 20, 2025) — first day of administration
  • FEMA stopped implementation: March 25, 2025
  • HUD: issued 1-year waiver Feb 21, 2025; pending rulemaking
  • EPA SRF rules: remain partially in effect pending separate rulemaking
  • Status: regulatory limbo; federal agencies no longer required to apply FFRMS to new projects
  • Implication: federally-funded housing, roads, bridges in floodplains may now be built to lower standard (BFE only) rather than FFRMS elevated standard
This is a live tension at any ASFPM event. Practitioners know FFRMS repeal increases long-term federal costs — but the current administration frames it as regulatory relief. Be prepared to quantify the cost of lower standards with NIBS data.

Risk Rating 2.0 — Detailed Policy Analysis

RR2.0 Design, Impact, Legal Challenge
  • Pricing variables: (1) Cost to rebuild (RCV), (2) distance to nearest water feature, (3) foundation type, (4) ground elevation vs. flood frequency sources, (5) flood type (coastal, riverine, pluvial)
  • Actuarial goal: Reflect actual property-level risk rather than FIRM zone proxy
  • Transition: New policies Oct 1, 2021; renewals Apr 1, 2022
  • Policy count impact: 11–39% drop nationally by late 2023; states with highest rate increases (coastal) saw largest drops; coverage gap is growing
  • Glide path: Existing policyholders capped at 18%/yr increases; some properties face 20–40 years of increases to reach full risk rate
  • Louisiana lawsuit (E.D. La.): State + parishes challenge RR2.0 as APA violation (arbitrary, failed to consider economic impacts); preliminary injunction denied; case ongoing
  • Congressional response: Bipartisan bills to cap increases at 9%/yr or freeze RR2.0; none enacted
  • Coverage paradox: As policies lapse (11–39% drop), federal disaster relief exposure increases — people without insurance require more federal assistance post-disaster

WS1 Sharp Questions for Geospatial Workshop

WS1 · Q1 · High Impact
BLE BFEs can over- or under-predict by 1–3 feet without surveyed channel bathymetry. When a community acts on BLE as if it's a detailed study — setting freeboard, triggering permits — and the BLE is wrong by 2 feet, who bears the liability? Has FEMA published any guidance on BLE's acceptable use in permitting decisions?
Why sharp: Forces acknowledgment of BLE's known limitations and the dangerous gap between "non-regulatory" label and actual community use.
WS1 · Q2 · High Impact
With the NAVD88 → NAPGD2022 transition expected mid-2026, every BFE and elevation certificate in this country will eventually need to be re-referenced. Are AECOM's current studies being dual-referenced? What does FEMA's transition plan look like for the 90%+ of population coverage in the NFHL?
Why sharp: Datum transition is the biggest infrastructure-level technical disruption in U.S. mapping in 30 years. Asking this signals you understand the full chain of consequences.
WS1 · Q3 · Technical
In 2D HEC-RAS models, mesh resolution at bridge crossings and culverts is notorious for creating numerical instability and inaccurate headwater. How does your shop handle the 1D-2D transition at structures, and what's your protocol for validating culvert headwater predictions against observed events?
Why sharp: This is a practitioner-level technical question that separates people who run models from people who understand them.
WS1 · Q4 · Policy
The ASPRS 2024 standard raised minimum checkpoints from 20 to 30. FEMA's lidar acceptance spec still references ASPRS 2014 in many contract vehicles. Are surveys delivered under old FEMA task orders being re-assessed against ASPRS 2024, or are communities accepting new FIRM panels based on outdated accuracy verification?
Why sharp: Exposes a contractual gap between evolving professional standards and FEMA procurement cycles.
WS1 · Q5 · Forward-looking
FFRD probabilistic products would give communities flood risk information from 5-year to 2,000-year return periods. But the NFIP's regulatory framework is binary — you're either in the SFHA or you're not. What legislative or rulemaking changes would be needed before FFRD products could replace the current FIRM system, and what's the realistic timeline?
Why sharp: Forces concrete discussion of the gap between where data science is going and where regulation currently sits.
WS1 · Q6 · Community-focused
For small communities with ≤5,000 population that can't afford a detailed engineering study, what's the gap in data quality between what BLE provides and what they actually need for sound permitting decisions? Is there a cost-effective middle path between BLE and full HEC-RAS 2D?
Why: Opens discussion on the equity of floodplain mapping — larger cities get detailed studies, small communities rely on BLE.

WS2 Sharp Questions for Nonstructural Workshop

WS2 · Q1 · The Key Question
44 CFR 60.3 prohibits dry floodproofing of residential structures in the SFHA — full stop. Given that ANSI/FM 2510 barriers are increasingly capable, and pluvial flooding gives homeowners only minutes of warning with no FIRM protection, is there any active FEMA rulemaking to revisit this prohibition? What would the failure mode analysis need to show for the prohibition to be reconsidered?
Why sharp: Gets to the core unresolved tension in the workshop's subject matter. David Elvir (US Flood Control) likely wants this prohibition lifted. Force the evidence question.
WS2 · Q2 · Equity
HMGP and FMA acquisition programs are voluntary and compensate owners at pre-flood fair market value. But renters — who often have less financial resilience — lose housing without compensation. Under Swift Current, is there any provision for tenant displacement assistance? If not, are "successful" buyouts simply displacing flood vulnerability onto renters who land in another affordable-but-flood-prone property?
Why sharp: The equity dimension of buyouts is rarely addressed technically. Forces the panel to engage with the full population impact.
WS2 · Q3 · BCA Technical
FEMA's BCA Toolkit uses a 7% discount rate per OMB Circular A-94. For a flood mitigation project with a 50-year life, that means a $1M benefit in year 30 is worth only $131K today. The UK's HM Treasury uses 3.5% for long-term projects. Has FEMA or ASFPM formally advocated for a lower discount rate for climate adaptation investments, given the intergenerational nature of the benefits?
Why sharp: The discount rate is a policy choice disguised as an accounting rule. A 3% rate would change the BCR of many currently ineligible projects from <1.0 to >1.0.
WS2 · Q4 · EWS Reality Check
The Texas Hill Country floods killed 139+ people despite having gauge networks. For urban pluvial events, catchment concentration times can be under 30 minutes from rainfall onset to damaging flow. What is the minimum viable EWS lead time for a homeowner to successfully deploy a 3-foot flood barrier? Has any presenter quantified the deployment time of ANSI/FM 2510-compliant systems against real-world urban flash flood warning times?
Why sharp: Forces honest acknowledgment that EWS + deployable barriers may not work for the fastest-onset events.
WS2 · Q5 · ASCE 24-24 Adoption
ASCE 24-24 mandates ANSI/FM 2510 compliance and annual deployment drills for deployable barriers. But ASCE 24-24 isn't in the 2024 I-Codes — it won't be until the 2027 cycle at the earliest. In the meantime, if a local jurisdiction adopts ASCE 24-24 independently for a floodproofing certificate, and the deployable barrier fails during a flood, who is the responsible party — the building owner, the barrier manufacturer, the local building official?
Why sharp: Liability chain for flood barrier failure is genuinely unresolved. Forces practitioners to think about risk transfer, not just risk reduction.
WS2 · Q6 · BRIC / Funding
With BRIC applications due July 23, 2026 and "ready-to-implement" infrastructure prioritized, what constitutes sufficient project readiness under the current NOFO? Is preliminary engineering enough, or do applicants need completed design documents to be competitive?
Why: Highly practical question that may save a community months of wasted application preparation.

Argument Bank

Evidence-backed claims for discussion — cite numbers, not opinions

WS1 Geospatial Arguments

BLE Limitations
BLE is being used as a de facto regulatory tool without the validation it requires.
BLE BFEs can deviate 1–3 ft from detailed study BFEs in watersheds without surveyed channel bathymetry. Yet communities with BLE but no effective FIRM are making permitting decisions based on it. FEMA labels BLE "non-regulatory" but provides no guidance on permissible uses in local ordinance administration.
Datum Transition Risk
The NAVD88 → NAPGD2022 transition will require re-evaluation of virtually every elevation certificate and BFE in the country.
NAVD88 carries a ~0.5 m national bias and ~1 m coast-to-coast tilt. Properties certified as "above BFE" today may shift status after transformation — or vice versa. FEMA has not yet published a transition roadmap for the NFHL, FIRMs, or elevation certificates.
Mapping Coverage Gap
The NFHL's >90% population coverage statistic conceals massive accuracy heterogeneity.
A 1990 flood study in a rural county and a 2024 2D HEC-RAS study in an urban corridor are both "in the NFHL." The 90% number counts both. Meanwhile, pluvial flooding — the dominant urban risk — has essentially zero FIRM coverage anywhere in the U.S.
Calibration Standards
Single-event calibration is insufficient for flood mapping models used to set regulatory BFEs.
Professional hydraulic modeling practice (HEC-RAS guidance, FEMA MH+T) recommends ≥2 independent events for calibration. Many FEMA-funded models are calibrated to one observed event, sometimes with limited observed WSE data, and then used to set BFEs that affect property values and insurance rates for decades.

WS2 Nonstructural Arguments

Residential Dry Floodproofing Prohibition
The blanket prohibition on residential dry floodproofing is a regulatory artifact that hasn't kept pace with barrier technology or pluvial flood risk realities.
44 CFR 60.3 was written when "floodproofing" meant masonry wall sealing — leak-prone and failure-prone. ANSI/FM 2510 (2020) establishes performance standards for deployable barriers to 3+ ft. No rulemaking to revisit the residential prohibition is pending. Meanwhile, pluvial urban flooding — not regulated by FIRMs — is the fastest-growing urban risk, leaving homeowners with no compliant active mitigation option.
Buyout Equity
HMGP/FMA voluntary buyouts, as implemented, systematically disadvantage renters and low-income homeowners.
Renters receive no compensation under current buyout programs; only property owners are paid. Pre-flood fair market value in flood-prone neighborhoods is often below replacement cost of comparable housing, because flood risk is already discounted into property prices. "Successful" buyouts may simply relocate flood vulnerability to other affordable-but-flood-prone areas.
BCA Discount Rate
FEMA's 7% BCA discount rate systematically under-values long-lived mitigation investments, making cost-effective projects appear infeasible.
At 7%, a $1M benefit in year 30 is worth $131K today. At 3% (UK HM Treasury standard for long-lived infrastructure), it's $412K. The choice of discount rate is a policy decision — not an actuarial truth. Many BRIC-eligible community projects that would pass a 3% BCR test fail at 7%. The NIBS $7:1 median BCR for riverine flood mitigation was calculated at the 7% rate — meaning the true societal return is higher.
CRS Equity
The CRS systematically rewards communities with administrative capacity, not necessarily those with the greatest flood risk or mitigation need.
~1,500 of 22,700+ NFIP communities participate in CRS. Class 1–4 (35–45% discount) = <50 communities. The documentation burden required for high CRS class is feasible only for well-staffed, well-funded planning departments — typically larger, wealthier communities. Smaller, lower-income, higher-risk communities that most need premium relief are least equipped to achieve meaningful CRS class.
Pluvial Flooding Gap
Pluvial flooding is the fastest-growing urban flood risk and is essentially unmapped by the current FIRM system.
~25% of NFIP claims come from outside the SFHA — many attributable to pluvial flooding. Urban catchment concentration times under 30 minutes. Storm sewer systems designed for 2–10 year storms are overwhelmed by 100-year events. FEMA has no systematic methodology for pluvial FIRM delineation. Milwaukee August 9–10, 2025 was primarily pluvial — most damaged properties were not in the SFHA.

Strategic Foresight

Five scenarios shaping floodplain management 2026–2035

Scenario 1
NFIP Reform: Comprehensive Reauthorization (Low Probability, High Impact)

Congress passes NFIP reform bill before September 30, 2026 expiry: means-tested assistance, mandatory coverage expansion, private market competition framework, and FFRD as future regulatory baseline.

Implications: Risk Rating 2.0 modified or frozen. Private market growth. FIRM replacement with probabilistic products accelerated. CRS reformed to reduce documentation burden. FEMA mapping budget potentially increased.

Conference angle: Ask presenters: "If comprehensive reform passes, what's the first thing you'd change about mapping methodology?"

Scenario 2
NFIP Muddles Through: Another Short-Term Extension (High Probability)

Congress passes 36th short-term extension; no comprehensive reform; Risk Rating 2.0 continues; policy count continues declining; fiscal deficit grows. Status quo for floodplain mapping, BRIC litigation continues.

Implications: Community frustration deepens. Practitioners at this conference will be managing the same structural tensions in 2027. Private flood insurance fills some gap. Federal disaster relief becomes de facto substitute for NFIP.

Conference angle: "Given this is almost certainly scenario, how are communities building resilience independent of federal program stability?"

Scenario 3
FEMA Review Council Restructuring Enacted (Medium Probability)

RAPID, R3P, FAIR programs enacted legislatively. Parametric block grants replace project-by-project PA. HMGP replaced by two-phase R3P. Disaster declaration thresholds raised.

Implications: Fewer presidential disaster declarations. States absorb more cost. Communities that can't document damage quantitatively get lower RAPID grants. Floodplain management programs may be decoupled from disaster assistance more cleanly.

Conference angle: Milwaukee's PA denial was under the current system. Under RAPID, would Milwaukee have gotten more or less? The parametric formula matters enormously.

Scenario 4
Datum Transition Disruption (Certain, Timeline Uncertain)

NAPGD2022 is adopted mid-2026 (FGCS vote). NAVD88-based products begin mandatory transition. Elevation certificates, FIRMs, BFEs all need transformation.

Implications: Properties near BFE with NAVD88 ECs may gain or lose compliance status. Litigation potential when property owners find their "above BFE" status changes. FEMA needs 5–10 year transformation plan. Surveying community has both a crisis and an opportunity.

Conference angle: "Is FEMA planning to grandfather existing ECs, or will every EC need to be retranscribed to NAPGD2022 upon adoption?"

Scenario 5
Pluvial Flood Mapping Initiative (Aspirational, 5–10 years)

Congress or FEMA launches systematic urban pluvial flood mapping program — analogous to Risk MAP but for stormwater/pluvial risk. Probabilistic depth grids replace SFHA delineation for urban areas.

Implications: Fundamentally transforms what "flood zone" means for urban homeowners. ~25% of current "outside SFHA" claims would shift to insurable zones. Would require NFIP reform (mandatory purchase in pluvial zones?). MMSD's Fresh Coast 740 becomes a model.

Conference angle: Milwaukee's August 2025 flood — mostly pluvial, mostly outside SFHA — is the strongest possible argument for this initiative. Make this argument explicitly.

FYFederal Fiscal Year; October 1 – September 30
GISGeographic Information System; software/data ecosystem for spatial analysis; ArcGIS (Esri), QGIS (open source) are dominant platforms
GISPGIS Professional; GISCI certification; signifies professional-level GIS competency

NVANon-Vegetated Vertical Accuracy; lidar accuracy measured on hard surfaces; primary accuracy metric; RMSEz-based
OMB Circular A-94Office of Management and Budget guidance on discount rates for federal programs; sets 7% real discount rate for BCA
PAPublic Assistance; FEMA reimbursement program for local governments' disaster costs; proposed replacement: RAPID

QL0/QL1/QL2/QL3USGS 3DEP lidar quality levels; QL2 = minimum for FEMA mapping (≥2 pts/m², ≤10 cm RMSEz); QL1 preferred for floodplain mapping

RAPIDResponsive Aid for Public Infrastructure Disaster; proposed FEMA Review Council replacement for Public Assistance; parametric block grant
